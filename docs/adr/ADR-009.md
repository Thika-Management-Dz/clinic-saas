# ADR-009: License — MIT During Development, Switch to Elastic License 2.0 or BUSL-1.1 Before Commercial Launch

- **Status:** Accepted
- **Date:** 2026-07-06
- **Blueprint references:** §6.1, §15 (Roadmap Phase 2 §2.1.3)
- **Supersedes:** none
- **Superseded by:** none

## Context

The operator intends to **commercialize** the Clinic Management SaaS as a
paid SaaS for Algerian clinics. The development phase uses AI coding agents
on a $0 budget; the source code is shared with these agents and may end up
visible to contributors, integrators, and (eventually) paying customers who
audit the security posture.

Two competing pressures:

1. **Source visibility** — clinicians and compliance reviewers want to verify
   that the EMR handling their patients' health data is not phoning home, not
   backdoored, and implements the RLS / audit / encryption claims in the
   blueprint. Source visibility is a trust and compliance asset.
2. **Restrict production use** — once the SaaS is commercialized, a competitor
   (or a clinic) must not be able to self-host the codebase and undercut the
   operator's pricing without contributing back. MIT/Apache permit this;
   Elastic License 2.0 / BUSL-1.1 restrict it.

## Decision

Adopt a **two-phase licensing strategy**:

### Phase A — Development: MIT

- The repository is licensed under **MIT** during development.
- Rationale: maximum simplicity, maximum AI-agent compatibility (agents are
  trained on MIT-licensed code; no source-available license friction in
  tooling or contributions), maximum contributor friendliness.
- The `LICENSE` file contains the MIT license text. `package.json` declares
  `"license": "MIT"`.

### Phase B — Commercial launch: switch to Elastic License 2.0 **or** BUSL-1.1

- **Before commercial launch**, the operator will choose one of:
  - **Elastic License 2.0 (ELv2)** — permissive for most uses; restricts
    (a) providing the software to third parties as a hosted/managed service,
    and (b) circumventing license-key functionality.
  - **Business Source License (BUSL-1.1)** — restricts production use;
    converts to a permissive license (typically Apache-2.0 or MIT) after a
    defined change date (commonly 4 years).
- The switch is **documented in a new ADR** (ADR-010, to be created at
  commercial-launch time) that records:
  - Which license was chosen (ELv2 or BUSL-1.1) and why.
  - The change date (for BUSL-1.1) or the additional-use grant (for ELv2).
  - The list of files that change license (the entire repo unless a
    permissively-licensed subset is carved out).
  - The mechanism for downstream users who cloned under MIT to transition
    (MIT is irrevocable; the switch applies to **future** versions, not
    past forks — this is the standard model used by Redis, MongoDB, Sentry,
    HashiCorp, etc.).

### Third-party dependencies

The stack's third-party dependencies remain under their own licenses
(§8.1, §6.1): MIT (Next.js, NestJS, Better Auth, shadcn/ui, Drizzle's
client libs), Apache-2.0 (Drizzle ORM, Drizzle Kit), PostgreSQL License
(PostgreSQL), MIT (pgBackRest), FSL-1.1-ALv2 (PowerSync, auto-converts to
Apache-2.0 after 2 years), GPLv3 (Orthanc — used as a separate Docker
container, not linked into the NestJS binary; GPLv3 does not virally apply).

The Clinic Management SaaS's own license applies **only to its own source
code**, not to its dependencies.

## Consequences

**Positive**

- During development: MIT maximizes AI-agent productivity and contributor
  friendliness.
- Before commercial launch: ELv2 / BUSL-1.1 restricts competitors from
  self-hosting the codebase as a competing SaaS, while preserving source
  visibility for trust/compliance.
- Two-phase strategy is the **industry-standard pattern** (Redis, MongoDB,
  Sentry SDK, HashiCorp Terraform, CockroachDB all switched from permissive
  to source-available before/around commercialization).
- No license friction during the $0 development phase.

**Negative**

- A contributor who submits code under MIT during Phase A retains their MIT
  grant on their own contribution. The operator should obtain a
  **Contributor License Agreement (CLA)** from any non-trivial contributor
  before merge, granting the operator the right to relicense. For a
  solo-operator + AI-agent workflow this is mostly moot (AI-generated code
  has no author copyright claim in many jurisdictions), but the CLA template
  should exist before any external human contribution lands.
- Switching licenses is a ** communications and trust event** — existing
  users who cloned under MIT may feel the switch is a rug-pull. Mitigated by
  (a) announcing the switch in advance in `CHANGELOG.md` and the project
  README, and (b) documenting the rationale in the future ADR-010.

## Alternatives Considered

### MIT or Apache-2.0 forever

Maximum simplicity and contributor friendliness. Rejected because it permits
a competitor to self-host the codebase as a competing SaaS without
contributing back, undermining the operator's commercialization plan.

### Elastic License 2.0 / BUSL-1.1 from day one

Maximum protection from the start. Rejected because:
- Source-available licenses complicate AI-agent tooling (some agents treat
  non-OSI licenses conservatively and refuse to read or write the code).
- BUSL-1.1's "production use" restriction could prevent early pilot clinics
  from running the system in production during the development phase, which
  we want them to do.
- The two-phase approach defers the complexity to the moment it actually
  matters (commercial launch).

### Proprietary / closed source

Rejected: source visibility is a trust and compliance asset for a health-data
system; clinics and compliance reviewers want to read the code that handles
patient data. Closed source would be a market-disadvantage in a
post-ANPDP-authorization world.
